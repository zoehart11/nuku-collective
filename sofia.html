Sofia Heke

“How can fashion represent the fluidity of masculine and feminine energy?”

As a Māori designer I discovered that since pre colonisation, gender and sexual fluidity has always been in Aotearoa. Takatapui is an ancient Māori term meaning “intimate companion of the same sex” and today is used the same way queer is used. Evidence of sexual fluidity are seen in historical chants and carvings.

Using 100% recycled fabric, I wanted showcase this fluidity of the masculine and feminine thats always been in Māori culture - and how sensuality can be expressed through all identities without being linked to sexuality.

As I chose to express this concept through fashion design, I researched textile techniques and manipulations such as gathering, smocking, tucking, sculpture, and drapery. I wanted a technique that provided fluidity and sensuality in my collection. I discovered that the tucking technique allowed fluidity and sculptural movement with fabric, so I manipulated and implemented this technique into my five-piece collection.

“How can fashion represent the fluidity of masculine and feminine energy?”

Connection to the land for me grows each time I come to a new place with something new to offer and learn, but I draw a lot of energy from Te Wai Pounamu, having lived there for a good portion of my life. The land down there is heavy, with deep green blanketed all over and wild places in between. This strong, wild and diverse beauty of the land is where I find connection to my own strength and identity. I hope other wāhine can explore what these concepts / energies mean for them and their connection to the land through my work.

The main textile techniques I work with consist of freeform crochet, hand painting / energy scape painting and raranga / weaving. All of these techniques give me the opportunity to transfer energy between me and the piece, physically moving my hands across the fabrication with intention uncovering the layers, forming an identity within the piece. This creation of visual energy / concept is really important for my process as I often find it hard to articulate concepts and emotion clearly through words.

My creative practice works within Te Ao Māori, encouraging interaction with strong female energies, opening spaces for conversations around reclamation, celebration of body and identity; a journey for both creator and consumer.

Wāhine no te Whenua; Women of the Land, Is an exploration of whakapapa. Drawing from the deep spiritual and physical connection between women and the land, showing the fierce strength wahine hold and the power that can be exchanged through kinship with te whenua. Looking to Papatūānuku, channeling the mana and tapu within.

Nuku Collective acknowledges and respects ngā iwi Māori as the Tangata Whenua of Aotearoa and are committed to upholding the principles of the Treaty of Waitangi.
